What you can change during AEP

The Annual Enrollment Period (October 15 – December 7) is the one time every year when almost any Medicare change is on the table. Here's what you're allowed to do:

  • Switch between Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage. Move from Original Medicare (Parts A and B) into a Medicare Advantage plan, or leave Medicare Advantage and go back to Original Medicare.
  • Change from one Medicare Advantage plan to another. If a different plan in your area fits your doctors, drugs, or budget better next year, you can move to it.
  • Add, drop, or switch your Part D drug coverage. Join a stand-alone Part D plan, drop the one you have, or switch to a different one. Comparing drug plans each year matters — nationwide, stand-alone Part D plan choices fell from 474 in 2025 to 367 in 2026.

Any change you make during AEP takes effect January 1, 2027. Your current plan keeps running until then.

AEP and the MA OEP are not the same window

AEP (October 15 – December 7) is open to everyone with Medicare, and you can make as many changes as you like — only your last choice counts. The Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31, 2027) is different: it's only for people already enrolled in a Medicare Advantage plan, and it allows just one change — switch to another MA plan, or drop back to Original Medicare (and add a Part D plan). If you're on Original Medicare on January 1, the MA OEP is not for you.

How the two big numbers connect

The reason this calendar feels crowded is that your raise and your plan are decided in the same few weeks. Your COLA depends on inflation measured over the summer; your Medicare choices depend on files CMS publishes in early fall. By the time you're comparing plans in November, you already know your raise — so you can weigh both at once.

Two things decide what actually reaches your bank account: the COLA itself, and your Medicare Part B premium, which is usually withheld straight from your Social Security check. When Part B rises, it quietly claws back part of the raise. That's the gap our COLA calculator is built to show you.

Frequently asked questions

When will I actually see the bigger check?

The {{COLA_PROJECTED_YEAR}} COLA is announced on {{COLA_ANNOUNCE_DATE_LONG}}, but the higher amount doesn't arrive right away. The new benefit takes effect for January 2027, which most people receive in their payment in January 2027. You'll get a notice in your my Social Security message center in late November, and a mailed notice in December, confirming your new amount before it starts.

What if I miss the Annual Enrollment Period?

If you miss AEP (October 15 – December 7), your current coverage simply carries into {{COLA_PROJECTED_YEAR}} with whatever changes your plan made — so it's worth reading your ANOC even if you plan to stay put. If you're already in a Medicare Advantage plan, you get one more chance during the MA Open Enrollment Period, January 1 – March 31, 2027, to switch plans or return to Original Medicare. Outside those windows, you generally need a Special Enrollment Period, which is triggered by specific life events like moving. Check your options on the official Medicare Plan Finder or by calling 1-800-MEDICARE.

Is the {{COLA_PROJECTED}}% COLA for {{COLA_PROJECTED_YEAR}} final?

No. The {{COLA_PROJECTED}}% figure is an early estimate from groups like The Senior Citizens League and AARP, based on inflation data so far. By law, the official COLA is the percentage increase in the average CPI-W for July, August, and September over the same quarter of the last year a COLA took effect, rounded to the nearest tenth. The Social Security Administration announces the official number on {{COLA_ANNOUNCE_DATE_LONG}}. For comparison, the confirmed {{COLA_CONFIRMED_YEAR}} COLA was {{COLA_CONFIRMED}}%.

Where do these dates come from?

All of them come from public government sources: the Social Security Administration and Bureau of Labor Statistics for the COLA, and CMS for the Medicare enrollment periods and plan files.
